About

This team-taught, introductory course provides a foundation for most additional coursework in the Art and Technology Studies department. Students are given a broad interdisciplinary grounding in the skills, concepts, and hands-on experiences they will need to engage the potentials of new technologies in art making. Every other week, a lecture and discussion group exposes students to concepts of electronic media, perception, inter-media composition, emerging venues, and other issues important to artists working with technologically based media. Students gained hands-on experience with a variety of forms and techniques central to technologically-based art making.


Students developed a "coding" sketchbook using p5js where they explored the ways that composition, animation, and interaction can be vehicles hardware such as web cameras, microphones, touch sensors and arduino controlled circuits to investigate subjects such as surveillance, data collection, collaboration, individual vs collective behaviors, 2D vs 3D, virtual vs physical, digital vs analog, etc.


As the global outbreak of covid19 brought classes online, these research topics have become ever more pertinent. We are very pleased to showcase a collection of projects here that include final projects in addition to curated prototypes, playful investigations, and leaps of faith. We encourage you to explore, interact, engage, and take pleasure here among the hard work they contributed to this online exhibition.

INTERACTIVE GAME

Interactive "game" that counts your score.

The point of the game is to click on the circle before it disappears and changes location. 

https://editor.p5js.org/aniqb/present/mvBAMWw1S 

 

This game is infinite on purpose and will only restarts if the whole browser is reloaded. This is because the point of this game is not to win or to loose but to keep pressing the "button".

  • The code operates like a circuit board where if one of the elements doesn't cooperate as it should be, then the whole game will not be functional.
  • It is also like a button, because when you are able to "catch" the blue circle, the code will add another point to your score. 
  • The circuit opens and closes every time that the blue"button" is pressed.

Puzzle of Positivity

Description:

The title of this piece is Puzzle of Positivity. This project/experiment is meant to spread positivity and good vibes. What better way to do that than with compliments and words of affirmation? Compliments and words of affirmation can boost your mood when you’re having a rough time and quarantine has been tough on everyone. I wanted to establish healthy habits as I personally find myself making comments that are self-deprecating and it’s something I’m trying to stop doing. When given a phrase, you are to recite it to yourself every morning for a week to give yourself a boost for the day ahead. It’s also a reminder to be kind to yourself and show compassion, as we can be our own worst critics. You will also be sent an image of the mystery puzzle piece associated with the number you picked to receive your phrase. The puzzle piece is a fun visual and a representation that this compliment is now a piece of you. It’s a building block in the puzzle of life and shows how we are all connected even in quarantine.

Lantern Project

by Cathy Zhang

For this project, I initially planned on using six lanterns for which when the audience walks by each one of them, they will light up as if a "spirit" is guiding or protecting them. In order to fulfill my plan, I decided on using an ultrasonic sensor, a type of distance sensor, to monitor the distance between the person and the lanterns and send orders to the Arduino board and Arduino will send the order to p5js, which will trigger the lanterns to light up and show the effect that the lanterns will light up when someone walks by. The work is shown in the p5js& ultrasonic sensor video. As you will see, when there is an object in front of the sensor within 2 cm and 50 cm (the 2 cm was planned for the sensor to be in a wall and disguised as part of the wall) the red light of the lantern would light up.

"Untitled" by Cher Feng

 

 

This untitled piece is a handshake between an Arduino Uno potentiometer circuit and p5.js code that allows the viewer to change the frequency at which the webcam feed is distorted. The live feed, at the baseline, is the video itself, but it juggles between that visual and the visual of other smaller fragments of the live feed collaged on top of itself, additionally with light distortion. The viewer may build their own potentiometer circuit and change the serial port name in line 3 to match that of their own in order to use their webcam and experience the handshake itself.

Translation

This is a passageway from a microphone to its visualization based on its volume and frequency. It begins by picking up sounds from a contact mic (a type of microphone that requires direct contact with a surface to pick up its vibrations), to an analog amplifier circuit (to boost the gain since contact mics can be very quiet). This is then fed into my p5.js sketch that rearranges the signal using a FFT (Fast Fourier transform) algorithm. This splits the sonic signal into frequency bands at their corresponding volumes to produce a visual representation like this one. It is a universal tool that can be applied with any kind of microphone, instrument or soundscape as the code runs on the frequency spectrum of the sound inputed. I hope to continue adapting and using this tool as a means of visual expression through sonic imagery.

Technological advancement and Regression... as a Circuit

(a sculptural display [and performance?] by yours truly)

When it comes to charging your phone it seems that even the companies that tout high design prowess have unfortunately left little thought to making the inevitable charging experience any more enjoyable with the general 3-foot cable supplied with virtually all smartphones. This coupled with the lack of outlets of convenient outlets in many places poses a less than user-friendly experience contrary to the general ethos that most tech companies would like to display. I attempted to solve the problem of inconvenient phone charging outlets with a common tool that's already made: extension cords. After looking at some sculptures from The KTDuffy Project I was inspired to create some readymade sculptures involving making a few of my outlets more accessible using online extension cords and adapters.

 

 

The new objects worked well as they created a central location in the middle of the room in which I could plug my phone inform many different angles and charge my phone while I used it in comfort with the provided 3-foot cable it came with. It quickly dawned on me that I would have to make these types of sculptures for inaccessible plug in my house and while they may have been fun to make there was still the issue of having the struggle to plug them in in the first place. At that point, the solution dawned on me what if you only ever had to plug your phone once and never have to go without charge again...

This work represents one of my understanding of a circuit, it’s a runway I built around my kitchen for a mini pool ball, it was supposed to end where it started, to represent the nature of a circuit, however, I found that was too difficult to achieve, so I changed it into this. The runway starts at the top of the refrigerator, after a steep acceleration the ball hits the first turning point, then drive on a section across the sink, that’s when it hits the second turning point, and then it would rides on a highway across the gap between tables, after glides smoothly through the table, it meets another accelerate section and finally hits the the king of spades and drops into the boots cup. The process of making the runway and adjusting it was a lot harder than I thought, but I finally made it and the drop sounds gorgeous.

Wire Tree

For my project I designed and created a wire tree that rests on top of a salt rock that also lights up. 

Materials

  • aluminum wire
  • Bucket
  • Drill
  • Plyers
  • Wire cutters
  • Salt lamp
  • tape/glue

 

Process

I started off by taking the wire and wrapping it around the bucket until I had enough to work with. The reason for the bucket is to make sure every strand is roughly the same size and to hold all the wire in place while I work. Once I have enough wire I start to feed it through holes that were pre drilled on the bottom of the bucket. Once every strand is placed I twist the wire to get a shape for the trunk. I then start to separate multiple strands so that I can form the branches. After that I carefully roll all the strands to make the leaves. Once this is all done I take the tree out of the bucket and separate sections of the bottom wire almost like I did for the branches. This is for the roots that will be used to hold the tree together with the salt lamp. Lastly I place the tree on top of the salt lamp, add some epoxy glue and then some tape to make sure it holds. I then plug it in and it's finished.

CONCEPT:

This idea was formed from an observation I had about my younger brother’s childhood in comparison to mine. He is constantly stimulated by his ipad which contains numerous benefits of educational learning through the internet or, in his case, fun Youtube videos. 

But I thought of the material of the iPad— the sleek titanium back with an aluminosilicate glass— and I thought of how much it deprives the user of the sensation of children’s books; the joy of physically handling different types of material. 

I cherished the way the pages felt, the illustrative touch, the smell of the book, or the surprise element from a pop-up book. Experiencing the different types of craft is something I feel like my younger brother misses out on. 

 

PROJECT:

In thinking of how to transform a storybook, I thought of embedding LCD screens to provide an animated gif to the pages. This added element of technology can be exciting, but you can also experience a book by its original form. 

 

I downloaded a pdf of The Giving Tree, a popular children’s book by Shel Silverstein, and created animated gifs on Photoshop. 

I then coded the gifs onto p5js, which would then be activated through an LCD screen through Arduino. The user… or… reader(?)… can use a sensor on each page to activate the code with the animated gif.
